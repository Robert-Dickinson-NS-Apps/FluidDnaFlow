## Gaps / risks (the main opportunities)

### 1) “Lattice units” will confuse many users

You already call this out (good), but users still need help understanding:

* what characteristic length (L) you’re using,
* how Re is being computed (and why it might not match the slider exactly),
* whether “pressure drop” is normalized/scaled. ([Fluid DNA Flow][1])

### 2) Interpretation tooling is missing (likely)

Most CFD demos show pretty pictures but don’t *teach* users how to read them. You’re close. The next level is: **turn visualization into an explorable explanation**.

### 3) Stability/quality guardrails

At higher Re (or aggressive inlet velocity / low viscosity), you’ll see:

* numerical artifacts,
* spurious oscillations,
* stability breakdowns.
  If users hit those and don’t understand why, trust drops.

## Ideas: improvements that add a lot of value fast

### Quick wins (1–3 days)

1. **Add an always-visible legend + scale bars**

   * Colorbar for pressure/vorticity magnitude
   * Vector scale for velocity
   * Current min/max displayed next to the legend

2. **Tooltips that teach**

   * Hover over “Vorticity” → “curl of velocity; highlights rotation / eddies”
   * Hover over “Re” → show formula (even if normalized)

3. **Preset buttons**

   * “Creeping flow (Re~10)”
   * “Separation onset (Re~50–100)”
   * “Vortex street (Re~150–250)”
     This matches your own educational narrative (boundary layer, wake formation, Kármán vortex street). ([Fluid DNA Flow][1])

4. **Shareable permalink**

   * Encode parameter settings in the URL (`?re=150&nu=0.08&u=2.0&layer=vorticity`)
   * Great for teaching, demos, and feedback.

5. **“Quality meter”**

   * Simple indicator: “Stable ✅ / Borderline ⚠️ / Unstable ❌”
   * Backed by heuristics (Courant-like criteria or LBM stability constraints).

---

### Medium scope (1–3 weeks)

6. **Make the “Documentation” truly engineering-grade**
   Include:

   * LBM model details (D2Q9? relaxation time? viscosity relation)
   * boundary conditions (inlet/outlet, bounce-back walls, obstacle BC)
   * what your “drag coefficient” definition is
   * how pressure is derived from density
     (Even a short “math appendix” helps you get taken seriously.) ([Fluid DNA Flow][1])

7. **Plot panels**
   Add live plots for:

   * drag coefficient vs time
   * pressure drop vs time
   * maybe a simple FFT to estimate shedding frequency / Strouhal number at higher Re
     This turns it from “cool” to “diagnostic.”

8. **Interactive geometry**

   * Click to place obstacles (circle, rectangle, airfoil-ish)
   * Or upload a simple SVG silhouette
     A geometry sandbox dramatically expands use cases.

9. **Export**

   * CSV of velocity field at timestep N
   * PNG snapshot with legend
   * short MP4/GIF export of a time window
     This is important for real workflows.

### Bigger bets (1–2 months)

10. **GPU acceleration (WebGL / compute shaders)**
    LBM is a great candidate for GPU. If you can jump from “runs” to “buttery smooth,” adoption jumps too.

11. **Validation mode**
    Include one or two classic validations:

* Poiseuille flow (known analytic profile)
* flow past cylinder drag coefficient in a narrow regime
  Give users a “this is real” anchor.

## If your intent is stormwater / Autodesk-adjacent (how to pivot this into something *very* relevant)

Your current app is essentially “flow field intuition + interactive simulation.” That’s *exactly* what stormwater tools often lack: **fast, visual, intuitive 2D surface flow understanding**.

Here are 6 high-leverage pivots:

1. **Swap “inlet flow” for “rainfall intensity + duration”**

   * Let users define a hyetograph
   * Show evolving depth + velocity over time (inundation animation)

2. **Terrain grid import**

   * Import a raster DEM or a simple heightmap
   * Your grid solver becomes “overland flow on terrain” (a stormwater goldmine)

3. **Add infiltration + roughness**

   * Minimal models: constant infiltration, Horton, Green-Ampt
   * Roughness slider (Manning’s n surrogate)
     This bridges CFD visuals into hydrology/hydraulics.

4. **Stormwater “objects”**

   * Inlets/catch basins as sink points
   * Culverts as constrained conduits
   * Detention basins as storage cells
     Then compute “captured vs bypassed runoff” and overflow depth maps.

5. **Scenario compare**

   * Baseline vs “add LID here” vs “bigger inlet spacing”
   * Show delta maps (peak depth reduction, time-to-drain)

6. **Autodesk workflow hook (conceptually)**

   * Import a surface + curb lines from a civil model
   * Export: inundation extents + peak depths back as layers for review
     Even without full hydraulic network solving, this becomes a **design review / communication** tool that complements traditional pipe-network modeling.
